While you sleep, your brain and body stay active. Sleep is a natural process
that restores energy, supports learning and memory, and keeps you healthy. Your
brain cycles through stages of non-REM and REM sleep through the night, and you
need all of them for good rest.

**What sleep does for you**

- Helps you feel rested and energetic the next day
- Supports learning, insight, and memory
- Gives your heart and blood vessels a rest
- Releases hormones for growth, muscle repair, and tissue healing
- Helps balance the hormones that control hunger and fullness
- Strengthens your immune system

**How much you need**

The amount depends on age, lifestyle, health, and sleep patterns. General
guidance:

- Teens (13-18): 8-10 hours a day
- Adults (18+): 7-9 hours a day

During puberty, teens' internal clocks shift later, which clashes with early
school start times, so many teens don't get enough sleep. Quality matters as
much as hours. If your sleep is frequently interrupted, you might not get enough
of certain stages. Ask yourself: do you struggle to get up, have trouble
focusing during the day, or doze off? If so, your sleep habits may need work.

**When you don't get enough**

Short sleep does more than make you tired. It can dull your thinking, slow your
reactions, and make memory harder, which raises the risk of mistakes and
accidents. It can also affect your mood, bringing irritability, low mood, or
anxiety, and over time it's linked to higher risk of high blood pressure, heart
disease, and type 2 diabetes. It also magnifies the effect of alcohol.

**Getting better sleep**

- Go to bed and wake up at the same time every day
- Avoid caffeine, especially in the afternoon and evening
- Avoid nicotine
- Exercise regularly, but not too close to bedtime
- Avoid alcohol and large meals late at night
- Skip naps after 3 p.m.
- Relax before bed with a bath, reading, or calm music
- Keep your bedroom cool, dark, and quiet
- Remove distractions like TVs, computers, and phones
- Get sunlight during the day
- If you can't fall asleep for 20 minutes, get up and do something relaxing
- See a provider if trouble sleeping continues

If you work shifts, good sleep can be harder. It may help to nap and extend total
sleep time, keep the lights bright at work, limit shift changes, keep caffeine to
the first part of your shift, and block out sound and light with curtains or
earplugs during daytime sleep.
