topic: Depression
---

Depression is more than feeling sad or irritable for a few days. It can affect
how you think and feel and reach into everyday life, so that even basic
activities like sleeping, eating, or working become difficult. You may not know
why you feel this way. Getting help at the earliest sign tends to make symptoms
easier to manage.

**What shapes it**

A mix of genetic, biological, environmental, and psychological factors can
contribute, and sometimes there's no clear cause. It can happen at any age and
affect anyone. Other conditions like diabetes, cancer, or heart disease can occur
alongside it, and each can make the other harder.

**It doesn't look the same on everyone**

Depression doesn't always appear as sadness, and it can vary by age. A child may
seem more irritable than sad. Teens may act out or sleep excessively. In older
adults the signs may be less obvious. Men may appear angry or irritable rather
than sad.

**Common symptoms**

- Feeling sad or empty
- Losing interest in favorite activities
- Overeating, or not wanting to eat at all
- Sleeping too much, or not enough
- Feeling very tired
- Feeling hopeless, irritable, anxious, or guilty
- Aches, pains, headaches, or digestive problems
- Thoughts of death or suicide
- Shifting moods or behaviors
- Increasing use of alcohol or drugs
- Pulling away from family and friends

**What can help**

Support often includes talk therapy, done one-on-one or in a group, which can
help you recognize and shift troubling emotions, thoughts, and behaviors, and can
give you and your family education, skills, and coping strategies. Healthy
lifestyle changes such as regular exercise, a consistent sleep schedule, and
managing stress also carry long-term benefits.

If you, or someone you love, is depressed, reach out to a provider. Getting help
at the earliest sign matters. If you or someone you know has thoughts of hurting
themselves or attempting suicide, contact a local crisis line or emergency
service right away.
