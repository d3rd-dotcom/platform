topic: Media Literacy and Evidence
---

Media literacy is the practice of understanding how information is made,
distributed, framed, and interpreted. It helps a reader separate a claim from
the evidence offered for it and the action the creator wants the reader to take.

**Read the whole message**

Start with the claim. Then identify the source, audience, format, date, and
purpose. A research paper, a news story, a video, a personal post, and an
advertisement use different conventions. A source can be useful for one purpose
and weak for another.

Look for:

- The original study or dataset behind a summary
- The size and type of the sample
- The comparison and the time frame
- Missing context, uncertainty, and limits
- Language designed to trigger urgency, fear, status, or belonging

**Trace before you share**

Open the cited source. Check whether it actually measured the outcome in the
post. A study about a short-term survey result cannot automatically support a
claim about long-term behavior. A percentage without a denominator can sound
larger or smaller than it is. A chart can change the visual impression by
starting the vertical axis above zero or leaving out comparison groups.

Pause before sharing a surprising claim. Write one sentence describing what the
source supports and one sentence describing what it leaves unanswered. That
small distinction keeps curiosity, skepticism, and care in the same workflow.
