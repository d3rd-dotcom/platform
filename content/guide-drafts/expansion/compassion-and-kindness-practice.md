Compassion is responding to difficulty with care, clear attention, and a wish
to reduce unnecessary suffering. It can be directed toward yourself, another
person, or a whole group. Compassion includes warmth, but it also includes
accurate boundaries and useful action.

**Three parts of a compassionate response**

- Notice the difficulty without pretending it is smaller than it is.
- Use language that treats the person as worthy of care.
- Choose one action that supports safety, repair, or the next step.

For example, a student who missed an assignment could say, "I am disappointed,
and I can check the instructions, email my teacher, and plan the next hour."
That response names the problem, avoids a personal attack, and creates a
concrete action.

**A two-minute practice**

Think of a small difficulty from today. Describe it in one neutral sentence.
Then write what you would say to a friend in the same situation. Remove blame
from the sentence while keeping the facts. Finish with one action that is safe,
possible, and specific. Compassion can hold boundaries, name harm, and leave
each person responsible for their own choices.

**Compassion and boundaries**

Kindness can include saying no, asking for help, taking a break, or telling a
trusted adult about a serious problem. A caring response respects consent and
connects someone with support when danger may be present. If distress is severe,
lasts, or makes normal activities difficult, involve a qualified adult or health
professional.

Practice can change the words you use when facing mistakes. Evidence about
health outcomes requires careful study across people and settings. Treat this
practice as a way to test a different response and observe what happens next.
