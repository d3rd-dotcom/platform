Meditation is a family of practices that trains attention and awareness. Some
practices use the breath, a sound, a visual image, a phrase, or a set of body
sensations as an anchor. Mindfulness is one approach that pays attention to the
present moment with curiosity and less automatic judgment.

**What meditation trains**

Meditation gives you a way to observe how the mind produces thoughts, feelings,
memories, and plans. The practice is noticing what appears,
recognizing when attention has wandered, and choosing where to place attention
again. That return is the repetition that builds skill.

Three parts show up in many beginner practices:

- An anchor gives attention a steady object, such as breathing or sounds.
- Awareness notices thoughts, feelings, and sensations as they change.
- A return brings attention back after distraction, with a calm and practical
  attitude.

**A short practice**

Choose a comfortable position. Set a timer for two minutes. Notice the feeling
of one breath. When a thought arrives, silently label it "thinking" and return
to the next breath. If the body feels tense, open your eyes, change position,
or stop. A practice can be useful while seated, walking, stretching, or doing a
routine task.

**What the evidence says**

Research on meditation studies many different practices, teachers, programs,
and outcomes. Some reviews report possible benefits for stress, anxiety, and
sleep, while other findings remain mixed or preliminary. A result from one
program needs its own context before its findings apply to another meditation
practice. Use meditation as a skill to explore alongside care from a qualified
professional when care is needed.

Some people report uncomfortable experiences during meditation. Stop or adjust
the practice if it increases distress, and ask a trusted adult or health
professional for support when needed.

**Try it**

Practice for two minutes on three different days. Record the anchor you chose,
how often your attention wandered, and what helped you return. A repeatable
observation matters more than a perfect score.
