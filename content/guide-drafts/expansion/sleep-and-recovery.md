topic: Sleep and Recovery

Recovery is the process of restoring attention, energy, and capacity after work,
stress, learning, or exercise. Sleep is one part of recovery. Quiet time,
movement, food, social connection, and a change of task can also help a person
recover during the day.

**Why routines help**

The brain and body respond to repeated cues. A similar sleep and wake schedule,
a dimmer wind-down period, and a place used for sleep can make the transition
to rest easier. A routine supports practice and carries no moral grade. School schedules,
caregiving, jobs, health conditions, and noisy environments can change what is
possible.

**Design a recovery plan**

Choose one evening cue, one morning cue, and one daytime reset. Examples include
packing school materials before bed, opening the curtains after waking, or
taking a short walk between study blocks. Test one change for a week. Track the
time you began the routine, how easy it was to start, and how you felt during
the next important task.

Avoid treating a single good or bad night as proof. Sleep and energy vary for
many reasons. A simple log helps you see patterns without pretending to measure
every cause.

NIMH recommends making sleep a priority and keeping a schedule. If sleep
problems or distress persist and interfere with ordinary activities, talk with
a qualified health professional or trusted adult. This guide explains habits
for learning and reflection; diagnosis belongs to a qualified health professional.
