topic: Cognitive Biases and Decisions
---

A cognitive bias is a predictable pattern in judgment that can pull attention
or reasoning away from all the available evidence. Biases are part of human
thinking. Learning their names gives you a chance to slow down when a decision
matters.

**Common patterns**

- Confirmation bias makes supporting evidence feel more important than evidence
  that challenges an existing belief.
- Availability bias makes vivid or recent examples feel more common than they
  are.
- Anchoring makes an early number or idea influence later judgment.
- Present bias gives immediate rewards more weight than future consequences.

These labels describe context-sensitive patterns. The same person can reason
carefully in one setting and rush in another. Time pressure, stress,
missing information, group pressure, and the design of a choice can change what
feels obvious.

**A decision pause**

Write the choice in one sentence. List the goal, the options, the evidence for
each option, and what would change your mind. Ask for one disconfirming fact.
Separate the cost you know from the cost you are guessing. If the decision is
important, add time or ask someone you trust to review the reasoning.

For everyday choices, a quick decision can be sensible. For a high-stakes choice,
slowing down is a useful tool. A bias checklist cannot remove uncertainty, but
it can make hidden assumptions visible.
