Reading a research claim means tracing a sentence back to the study or data
behind it. A headline can be interesting and still leave out the sample, method,
comparison, uncertainty, or limits.

**Use a five-question check**

1. What exactly was measured?
2. Who took part, and how were they selected?
3. What was the comparison or baseline?
4. What size and direction was the result?
5. What limits did the researchers name?

"Students who used a breathing app felt calmer" needs more information. How many
students used it? Did they choose the app themselves? How was calm measured?
Was there a comparison group? How long did the change last? The answers decide
how much confidence the claim deserves.

**Evidence has levels of strength**

A personal story can show that an experience happened. A survey can show a
pattern in reported experiences. An experiment can compare conditions. A review
can combine results from multiple studies, but the review still depends on the
quality and similarity of the studies it includes. Each type answers different
questions.

NCCIH notes that meditation research includes many practices and outcomes, and
that some findings are preliminary or difficult to compare. That is a useful
model for reading wellness claims: ask what the evidence actually tested before
turning a possibility into a promise.

Write the claim again using a verb that matches the evidence: "was associated
with," "participants reported," or "the study tested." Accurate language keeps
curiosity alive while protecting the reader from overconfident conclusions.
