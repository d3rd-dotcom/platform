Study design is the plan connecting a question to observations. A strong design
defines the people, variables, comparison, timing, and limits before the data
arrive.

**Choose a method**

Case studies examine one person or a small number of cases in depth. Naturalistic
observation records behavior in a real setting while the researcher minimizes
interference. Surveys ask questions across a sample. Archival research examines
records already collected. Experiments introduce a planned change and compare
outcomes under controlled conditions.

Observational methods can reveal patterns and generate questions. They cannot
usually establish that one variable caused another. Experiments provide stronger
control over a planned change, but ethical limits and artificial settings can
affect what the results mean in daily life.

**Design a fair comparison**

Imagine testing a two-minute breathing practice before homework. Decide whether
the comparison group gets quiet sitting, another activity, or no instruction.
Use the same homework task and outcome measure for both groups. Record who
participated and who stopped. If the breathing group improves, other explanations
could include expectations, extra attention, or differences between the groups.

Ethics matter before efficiency. People need understandable information about
what will happen, a way to choose freely, privacy protections, and a plan for
responding to risk. A student project should use low-risk activities and follow
school rules with adult supervision.

The best design is the one that matches the question and makes its limits easy
to see. Study quality comes from clear choices, careful measurement, and honest
reporting.
