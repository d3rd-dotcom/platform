topic: Correlation and Causation
---

Correlation describes how two variables change together. Causation means a
change in one variable helps produce a change in another. A correlation can be
useful evidence for a relationship. A correlation alone rarely shows cause and
effect.

**Three explanations for a relationship**

Suppose students who sleep more report better focus. Sleep may help focus. Good
focus may make it easier to protect time for sleep. A third variable, such as
work schedule, health, stress, or access to a quiet room, may affect both.

When a study measures variables without assigning an intervention, researchers
can describe a relationship and possible explanations. They need a stronger
design to make a causal claim. Experiments change one condition, compare it with
another, and control as many other factors as practical. Even experiments can
have limits if the sample, measure, or setting differs from real life.

**Read a claim carefully**

Watch the verbs. "Linked with" and "associated with" describe a relationship.
"Caused" and "led to" make a causal claim. Ask whether the study design supports
the verb. Also check the size of the sample, the time order, the measurement,
and whether the result was consistent across people.

**Try a data table**

Write down five pairs of observations, such as minutes slept and a focus rating.
Look for direction and unusual points. You can say whether the pattern appears
positive, negative, or unclear. You cannot use five observations to prove a
general rule. The lesson is to keep the conclusion proportional to the data.
