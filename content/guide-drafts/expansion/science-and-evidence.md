topic: Science and Evidence
---

Science is a way to build and check explanations using observations, measures,
reasoning, and evidence. A scientific claim describes what the evidence supports
and leaves room for revision when better evidence arrives.

**Start with a question**

"Does music help me study?" is a useful curiosity. A researchable version names
the variables: "During a 20-minute reading task, does instrumental music change
the number of correctly recalled details?" The second question gives a team a
shared way to collect and compare observations.

**Separate parts of a claim**

- A measure is how an idea becomes an observation, such as correct answers or
  minutes focused.
- A sample is the smaller group observed in a study.
- A population is the wider group the researcher wants to understand.
- A method is the plan for collecting information.
- A conclusion states what the data support and how uncertain the result is.

Every method has strengths and limits. A case study can give rich detail about
one person. A survey can reach many people but depends on the questions and
the accuracy of self-report. Observation can show behavior in context. An
experiment can test a planned change but may use an artificial setting. The
right method depends on the question.

**Check the bridge from evidence to conclusion**

Ask who was measured, what was measured, how the comparison worked, and what
other explanations remain possible. Look for missing data, selection effects,
unclear definitions, and conclusions that reach beyond the sample.

Good science is a shared practice of making claims clear enough for another
person to inspect. That habit helps with wellness advice, social media claims,
and everyday decisions.
