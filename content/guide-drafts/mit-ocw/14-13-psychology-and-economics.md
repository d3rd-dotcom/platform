Behavioral economics is a subfield of economics that incorporates insights from psychology and other social sciences. It studies how human behavior systematically deviates from mainstream economic assumptions, with applications across labor, public policy, industrial organization, health, finance, and development economics. This is MIT's 14.13 course (Spring 2020, taught by Prof. Frank Schilbach), a survey of that field.

**Course basics**

Undergraduate and graduate level. Prerequisite: an introductory microeconomics course. Meets twice a week for lecture plus one weekly recitation.

**Topics covered, in order**

- Introduction and overview
- Time preferences (theory, then applications)
- Risk preferences
- Reference-dependent preferences
- Social preferences
- Limited attention
- Utility from beliefs and learning
- State-dependent preferences, projection, and attribution bias
- Gender, discrimination, and identity
- Defaults, nudges, and frames
- Malleability and inaccessibility of preferences
- Poverty through the lens of psychology
- Happiness and mental health
- Policy with behavioral agents
- Behavioral economics and COVID-19

**Grading**

Exams count for 70% (a 30% midterm and 40% cumulative final, with the final able to replace the midterm score). Problem sets count for 25% (five sets, lowest score dropped). In-class quizzes count for 5%.
