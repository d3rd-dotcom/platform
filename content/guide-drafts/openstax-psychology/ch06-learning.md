Some behavior is instinctive — a hatchling sea turtle knows how to find the ocean within minutes of being born, no help needed. Human behavior mostly isn't: skills have to be acquired through experience. This chapter looks at the core ways humans learn, with a focus on operant conditioning — how behavior changes based on its consequences.

**Core concept**

Operant conditioning is how organisms learn to associate a behavior with its consequence. Pleasant outcomes make a behavior more likely to repeat — for example, a dolphin gets a fish for performing a trick and performs it again.

**Key theorists**

B.F. Skinner built on Edward Thorndike's law of effect: satisfying consequences strengthen a behavior, unpleasant ones weaken it. Skinner ran experiments using the "Skinner box," a chamber where animals pressed levers or pecked disks to get food rewards.

**Reinforcement vs. punishment**

"Positive" means adding something; "negative" means removing something. Reinforcement makes a behavior more likely; punishment makes it less likely. All four combinations exist: positive reinforcement, negative reinforcement, positive punishment, negative punishment.

**Reinforcement types**

- Positive reinforcement: adding a desirable stimulus to increase a behavior.
- Negative reinforcement: removing an undesirable stimulus to increase a behavior (like a seatbelt warning sound stopping once you buckle up).
- Primary reinforcers: innately rewarding (food, water, shelter).
- Secondary reinforcers: rewarding only through association (money, praise, stickers).

**Shaping**

Shaping breaks a complex behavior into small steps, reinforcing each closer approximation of the target behavior. This matters because organisms rarely produce complex behaviors spontaneously.

**Reinforcement schedules**

- Fixed interval: reward comes at predictable time intervals.
- Variable interval: reward comes at unpredictable time intervals.
- Fixed ratio: reward comes after a predictable number of responses.
- Variable ratio: reward comes after an unpredictable number of responses — this schedule is the most resistant to extinction.

**Latent learning**

Edward Tolman's research showed that organisms learn even without immediate reinforcement, building "cognitive maps" of an environment they can use later.
