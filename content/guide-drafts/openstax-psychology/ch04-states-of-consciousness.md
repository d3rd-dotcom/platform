Consciousness is your awareness of internal and external stimuli — everything from hunger and emotions to sunlight and sound. It isn't fixed: it shifts across a continuum from full alertness to deep sleep, and some people also experience altered states through meditation, hypnosis, or substance use. This chapter focuses mainly on sleep: its stages, its disorders, and what disrupts it.

**What consciousness is**

Sleep is a state of relatively low physical activity and reduced sensory awareness. Wakefulness is the opposite: high sensory awareness and cognitive activity. Consciousness moves between these states rather than sitting at one fixed point.

**Circadian rhythms**

A circadian rhythm is a recurring, cyclical pattern of bodily change that repeats roughly every 24 hours, regulating your sleep-wake cycle in step with light and dark. Body temperature, heart rate, blood pressure, and blood sugar all fluctuate on this daily cycle.

The suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN), located in the hypothalamus, acts as the brain's biological clock. It receives light information directly from neurons in the retina. The pineal gland releases melatonin in response — darkness triggers release, light suppresses it — which helps set your circadian rhythm.

**Circadian disruptions**

People differ in their natural sleep-wake pattern, known as chronotype: some are morning people, some are night owls. Circadian rhythms get disrupted by jet lag (crossing time zones) and rotating shift work, both of which cause fatigue, irritability, and trouble sleeping. Bright light exposure can help reset the biological clock after a disruption.

**Sleep debt**

Sleep debt builds up when you chronically don't get enough sleep. It shows up as reduced alertness and mental efficiency. The National Sleep Foundation's recommended amount varies by age; adults generally need 7-9 hours a night. Chronic sleep deprivation is linked to obesity, high blood pressure, elevated stress, reduced immune function, and cognitive impairment comparable to alcohol intoxication.
