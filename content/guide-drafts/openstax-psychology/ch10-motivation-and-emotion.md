Emotions can trigger instant shifts in behavior — in a crisis, some people help, some flee, and the difference comes down to how emotion moves through us. This chapter covers what compels our actions and how universal (or culture-specific) our emotional experiences are, focusing here on the theory and biology of emotion.

**What emotion is**

An emotion is a subjective state of being — what we call a feeling. It combines subjective experience, expression, cognitive appraisal, and a physiological response. Emotions are distinct from moods: emotions are intense, conscious reactions to a specific stimulus, while moods are longer-lasting, less intense states without a clear trigger.

**Theories of emotion**

- James-Lange theory: emotional experience follows physiological arousal — you feel fear after your body has already responded to a threat.
- Cannon-Bard theory: physiological arousal and emotional experience happen at the same time, independently of each other.
- Schachter-Singer two-factor theory: emotion results from physiological arousal interpreted through cognitive context — identical physiological states can produce different emotions depending on how you label them.
- Lazarus's cognitive-mediational theory: emotions are determined by your appraisal of the stimulus, with the appraisal happening before the emotional response.

Related ideas: the facial feedback hypothesis, which suggests facial expressions can influence how intense an emotion feels; automatic emotion regulation (AER), which happens without deliberate thought; and constructivist theory, which frames emotions as predictions built from past experience rather than fixed, innate responses.

**Biological basis: the limbic system**

- Hypothalamus: activates the sympathetic nervous system's response.
- Thalamus: relays sensory information.
- Amygdala: processes emotional information — its basolateral complex handles classical conditioning and attaching emotional value to experiences; its central nucleus regulates autonomic and endocrine responses.
- Hippocampus: integrates emotional experience with cognition.

**Facial expressions and culture**

Seven facial expressions appear to be universal: happiness, surprise, sadness, fright, disgust, contempt, and anger. Cultural display rules still shape how often and how appropriately people express emotion, and these rules vary across cultures. Body language — posture and movement — also communicates emotional state.
