Stress comes from more than just exams and deadlines — money worries, relationship conflict, family obligations, time pressure, even small everyday friction like traffic or a lost item all add to overall strain. This chapter looks at how stress affects the body and mind, and what actually helps in managing it.

**Coping styles**

Coping is the mental and behavioral effort you use to deal with stress-related problems. Researchers Lazarus and Folkman identified two main approaches:

- Problem-focused coping: managing or changing the stressor itself — identifying the problem, weighing options, choosing a solution. Example: a student who's failing a class contacts their professor, sets a study schedule, and finds a tutor.
- Emotion-focused coping: changing or reducing the negative emotions tied to the stress, through avoidance, positive comparison, finding meaning, or reappraisal. Example: the same student watches movies or plays video games to distract themselves instead.

Problem-focused coping tends to dominate when the stressor is controllable; emotion-focused coping tends to dominate when it isn't. For uncontrollable situations, like grief, emotion-focused strategies tend to help more.

**Control and stress**

Perceived control is your belief in your own capacity to influence and shape outcomes, and it has a major effect on how you react to stress. Researcher Albert Bandura argued that the intensity and persistence of stress is governed largely by how much control a person believes they have over the demands in their life. Higher perceived control is linked to better physical and mental health, more psychological well-being, and lower reactivity to stressors.

Martin Seligman's experiments found that dogs given inescapable shocks later failed to escape shocks they could have avoided — a pattern he called "learned helplessness," a belief in one's own powerlessness. He theorized this pattern might explain how people respond to repeated uncontrollable negative events. A later refinement of the theory focuses on attributions — the explanations people give themselves for negative events. People who attribute poor outcomes to internal, stable, and global causes ("it's me, it's permanent, it affects everything") tend to be more vulnerable; attribution habits can shift with training. Higher perceived control is also linked to lower rates of heart disease and cardiovascular decline.

**Social support**

Social support is the soothing effect of friends, family, and acquaintances — advice, encouragement, acceptance, emotional comfort, and practical help. A landmark Alameda County study that followed nearly 7,000 people over nine years found that socially isolated individuals were 2.3 to 2.8 times more likely to die than socially connected peers, even after controlling for other health factors. A later review of 148 studies covering more than 300,000 participants found that people with stronger relationships had a 50% greater likelihood of survival than those with weaker ones — an effect on the scale of quitting smoking.

Social support is also linked to better survival after breast cancer, fewer colds, stronger immune function, and lower blood pressure during stressful tasks, especially when the support comes from friends. Reduced social support leaves students more vulnerable to anxiety, depression, and loneliness, which is part of why peer mentoring programs are useful.

**Stress and discrimination**

Discrimination acts as an uncontrollable, persistent, unpredictable stressor, with outsized health effects on people facing multiple forms of it. Repeated discrimination can drive the body's stress response toward exhaustion, contributing to depression, cardiovascular disease, and other health problems over time. Social support and healthy coping can buffer some of this impact, though legal protections and antidiscrimination efforts remain necessary on their own.

**Stress reduction techniques**

- Exercise: physically fit people handle stress better and recover from it faster; exercise may buffer the body's stress response and reduce stress's effects on memory.
- Relaxation response (developed by Herbert Benson): combines relaxation with meditation through four steps — sit comfortably, find a quiet space with eyes closed, repeat a mantra, and passively focus on pleasant thoughts. This reduces the body's stress arousal and can help with high blood pressure.
- Biofeedback (developed by Gary Schwartz): uses equipment that measures muscle and autonomic activity and feeds it back visually or audibly, letting a person practice voluntary control over normally involuntary processes. Used for tension headaches, high blood pressure, asthma, and phobias.
