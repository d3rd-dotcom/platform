Two people raised in the same household can turn out completely differently — one becomes a president, another struggles with the law. What internal forces shape those diverging choices? Personality psychology is the field built to examine questions like this. This chapter traces how ideas about personality have developed, from ancient theories through modern approaches.

**What personality is**

Personality refers to the long-standing traits and patterns that lead a person to consistently think, feel, and behave in specific ways. Each person has a distinctive, fairly stable pattern of characteristics that shapes how they interact with others and their environment. The word "personality" comes from the Latin *persona*, originally the term for the masks actors wore to project a character.

**Ancient and classical theories**

Hippocrates (370 BCE) theorized that personality came from four bodily temperaments: choleric (yellow bile), melancholic (black bile), sanguine (red blood), and phlegmatic (white phlegm). Galen later expanded this, proposing that personality differences and disease both resulted from imbalances among these humors, with one temperament dominant in each person.

**Phrenology**

In 1780, Franz Gall proposed that bumps on the skull revealed personality traits and mental abilities. The idea was popular for a time but was eventually discredited as pseudoscience for lack of empirical support.

**Later developments**

Immanuel Kant and Wilhelm Wundt built on Galen's four-temperament model. Wundt proposed describing personality along two axes — emotional/nonemotional and changeable/unchangeable — a more graded approach than sorting people into fixed categories.

**Modern theories**

Sigmund Freud developed the first comprehensive personality theory, from a psychodynamic perspective emphasizing unconscious drives and childhood experience. Neo-Freudian theorists modified his approach, putting less weight on sexuality and more on social and cultural influence. Later approaches added learning, humanistic, biological, evolutionary, trait, and cultural perspectives on personality.
