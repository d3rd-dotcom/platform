Cognitive psychology is the study of thinking: how people solve problems, how someone who's never touched snow can still understand what it is, how intelligence varies between people. This chapter covers the high-level cognitive processes that build on perception, learning, and memory — starting with what cognition itself is.

**What cognition is**

Cognition is thinking — it covers the processes involved in perception, knowledge, problem solving, judgment, language, and memory. Cognitive psychology studies how thinking, emotion, creativity, language, and problem-solving interact.

**Concepts and prototypes**

The brain organizes information into concepts: categories that group linguistic information, images, ideas, or memories. A prototype is the best example of a concept — a Golden Retriever, for instance, as the prototype for "dog."

**Natural and artificial concepts**

Natural concepts form through direct or indirect experience — you come to understand snow by seeing it or seeing it in films. Artificial concepts are defined by fixed characteristics that never change — a triangle always has three angles and three sides.

**Schemata**

A schema is a mental construct made of a cluster of related concepts. Two types:

- Role schemas: assumptions about how people in a given role tend to behave.
- Event schemas (cognitive scripts): routine sets of behavior, like the unwritten etiquette of riding in an elevator.

Schemata make information processing more efficient, but they can also lead to inaccurate assumptions about individuals.
