topic: Coping skills / resilience

A traumatic event is a shocking, scary, or dangerous experience that can affect
someone emotionally and physically. Examples include natural disasters, acts of
violence, and serious accidents. It is natural to feel afraid during and after
one; fear is part of the body's fight-or-flight response.

**Common reactions after trauma**

- Feeling anxious, sad, or angry
- Trouble concentrating and sleeping
- Continually thinking about what happened

Most people recover, and their reactions ease over time.

**Signs a person may need more support**

- Worrying a lot or feeling very anxious, sad, or fearful
- Crying often
- Trouble thinking clearly
- Frightening thoughts, flashbacks, or reliving the experience
- Feeling angry, resentful, or irritable
- Nightmares or difficulty sleeping
- Avoiding places or people that bring back the memories
- Becoming isolated from family and friends

Physical signs can also point to a need for support: headaches, stomach and
digestive trouble, difficulty sleeping, a racing heart and sweating, or being
very jumpy and easily startled.

**How children and teens may react**

Children can react strongly, but their signs may differ from adults. In children
younger than six, this can include bed-wetting after being toilet-trained,
trouble talking, acting out the event during play, or being unusually clingy.
Older children and teens usually look more like adults, and may also show
disruptive or destructive behavior, guilt, or thoughts of revenge.

**Healthy ways to cope**

- Avoid alcohol and drugs
- Spend time with trusted, supportive friends and relatives
- Keep routines for meals, exercise, and sleep
- Do exercise, mindfulness, or other stress-reducing activities
- Set realistic goals and focus on what you can manage

If you or someone you know is struggling or having thoughts of suicide, contact
a local crisis line or emergency service right away.
