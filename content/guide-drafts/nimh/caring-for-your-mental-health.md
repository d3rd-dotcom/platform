Mental health includes your emotional, psychological, and social well-being.
Self-care means taking time to do things that help you live well. Even small
acts add up, and they can help you manage stress, lower your risk of illness,
and boost your energy.

**Self-care that helps**

- Get regular exercise. Even 30 minutes of walking a day can lift your mood, and
  small amounts of activity add up.
- Eat regular, balanced meals and stay hydrated. Notice how caffeine and alcohol
  affect your mood.
- Make sleep a priority. Keep a schedule, and cut blue light from screens before
  bed.
- Try a relaxing activity like meditation, muscle relaxation, or breathing
  exercises. Schedule time for things you enjoy.
- Set goals and priorities. Decide what must get done now and what can wait, and
  say no when you're taking on too much.
- Practice gratitude. Name specific things you're grateful for.
- Notice and challenge negative or unhelpful thoughts.
- Stay connected with friends or family who can offer support.

Self-care looks different for everyone. It may take some trial and error to
find what works best for you.

**When to seek more support**

Consider talking to a health care provider if you're experiencing severe or
distressing symptoms that have lasted two weeks or more, such as:

- Difficulty sleeping
- Changes in appetite or unplanned weight changes
- Difficulty getting out of bed because of your mood
- Difficulty concentrating
- Loss of interest in things you usually enjoy
- Inability to complete usual tasks and activities
- Feeling irritable, frustrated, or restless

A primary care provider can point you toward the next steps.

If you or someone you know is struggling or having thoughts of suicide, contact
a local crisis line or emergency service right away.
