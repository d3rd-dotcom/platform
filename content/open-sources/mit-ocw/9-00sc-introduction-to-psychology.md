## 9.00SC Introduction to Psychology (Fall 2011)

Instructor: Prof. John D. E. Gabrieli, Department of Brain and Cognitive Sciences.

### Course Description

"This course is a survey of the scientific study of human nature, including how the mind works, and how the brain supports the mind."

The course examines the mental and neural foundations of perception, emotion, learning, memory, cognition, child development, personality, psychopathology, and social interaction. It explores fundamental questions regarding nature versus nurture, free will, consciousness, human differences, self, and society.

### Topics and Units (calendar order)

- Brain structure, functions, and research methods
- Vision and visual perception
- Consciousness and attention
- Memory systems and amnesia
- Learning processes
- Language and cognitive abilities
- Thinking and intelligence
- Emotion and motivation
- Personality psychology
- Child and adult development
- Stress and coping
- Psychopathology (mental disorders)
- Social psychology
- Evolutionary psychology and happiness

### Course Format

The program includes lecture videos, reading assignments (including a free online textbook), self-check questions, discussion materials, supplementary resources, and three exams with solution keys. Exams are not cumulative and consist of multiple-choice and short-answer questions based on text and lectures; at MIT, enrolled students also complete two substantial writing assignments analyzing scientific source readings on a controversial topic in psychology.

### Notable unit: Emotion & Motivation (Lecture 15)

Study materials reference Chapter 10, "Emotion and Motivations: Feeling and Striving," from Kosslyn & Rosenberg's *Psychology in Context*. Lecture content discusses the components of emotion, ways of expressing emotion, and the parts of the brain involved in emotional processing, regulation, and perception. A companion discussion page covers emotion theories including James-Lange, Cannon-Bard, and cognitive theories, plus the role of emotion in decision-making and learning.
