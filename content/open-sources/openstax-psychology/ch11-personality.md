### Chapter Introduction

The introduction presents a compelling case study contrasting two half-brothers raised in the same household. Despite shared upbringing in Hope, Arkansas, one brother became the 42nd U.S. President while the other faced repeated legal troubles involving substance abuse. This contrast illustrates the central question: "Why did they make the choices they did? What internal forces shaped their decisions?" The chapter positions personality psychology as the discipline equipped to examine such divergent life trajectories and understand the psychological mechanisms underlying human behavior and character development.

### 11.1 What Is Personality? (full section content)

**Definition**

"Personality refers to the long-standing traits and patterns that propel individuals to consistently think, feel, and behave in specific ways." Each individual possesses a distinctive pattern of enduring characteristics and interacts with others and their environment in a particular manner. These personality traits are generally considered stable over time and resistant to change.

The term "personality" derives from the Latin word *persona*, which originally referred to theatrical masks actors wore to represent or project specific character traits.

**Historical Perspectives**

*Ancient and Classical Theories*

Hippocrates (370 BCE) theorized that personality traits stem from four bodily temperaments: choleric (yellow bile), melancholic (black bile), sanguine (red blood), and phlegmatic (white phlegm). The Greek physician Galen later expanded this theory, proposing that personality differences and diseases resulted from humoral imbalances, with each person exhibiting one dominant temperament.

*Phrenology*

In 1780, Franz Gall proposed that skull bumps revealed personality traits and mental abilities. While initially popular, phrenology was eventually discredited and classified as pseudoscience due to insufficient empirical evidence.

*Later Developments*

Immanuel Kant and Wilhelm Wundt built upon Galen's four temperament model. Wundt proposed describing personality using two axes: emotional/nonemotional and changeable/unchangeable, offering a more nuanced framework than categorical classification.

*Modern Theories*

Sigmund Freud developed the first comprehensive personality theory from a psychodynamic perspective, emphasizing unconscious drives and childhood experiences. Neo-Freudians modified his approach, reducing emphasis on sexuality while highlighting social and cultural influences. Later perspectives include learning, humanistic, biological, evolutionary, trait, and cultural approaches.
