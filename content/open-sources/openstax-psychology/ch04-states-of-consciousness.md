### Chapter Introduction

The chapter explores how consciousness fluctuates throughout daily life. While waking hours involve heightened alertness and awareness, this changes dramatically during deep sleep and dreaming states. Some individuals also encounter altered consciousness through meditation, hypnosis, or substance use.

The material emphasizes sleep as a primary focus, identifying its different stages and associated disorders. Additionally, it addresses modified consciousness states induced by psychoactive drugs, hypnotic techniques, and meditative practices.

### 4.1 What Is Consciousness? (full section content)

**Learning Objectives:** Students will be able to understand consciousness, explain circadian rhythms' role in sleep-wake cycles and their disruptions, and discuss sleep debt concepts.

**Definition of Consciousness**

"Consciousness describes our awareness of internal and external stimuli." Internal awareness includes pain, hunger, thirst, sleepiness, thoughts, and emotions. External awareness encompasses sensory experiences like seeing sunlight, feeling warmth, or hearing voices.

Consciousness exists on a continuum from full awareness to deep sleep. The text distinguishes between sleep as "a state marked by relatively low levels of physical activity and reduced sensory awareness" and wakefulness, characterized by high sensory awareness and cognitive activity.

**Biological Rhythms and Circadian Cycles**

Biological rhythms are recurring, cyclical patterns of bodily changes. A circadian rhythm occurs "over a period of about 24 hours" and regulates sleep-wake cycles linked to environmental light-dark patterns. Body temperature, heart rate, blood pressure, and blood sugar all fluctuate daily.

The suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) in the hypothalamus functions as the brain's biological clock, receiving light information from retinal neurons. The pineal gland releases melatonin, stimulated by darkness and inhibited by light, regulating these biological rhythms.

**Circadian Disruptions**

Individual differences in sleep-wake patterns are termed chronotype—whether someone is a morning person or night owl. Disruptions occur through jet lag (traveling across time zones) and rotating shift work, both causing fatigue, irritability, and insomnia. Bright light exposure can help realign biological clocks.

**Sleep Debt**

Sleep debt occurs when people chronically lack sufficient sleep. Consequences include decreased alertness and mental efficiency. The National Sleep Foundation recommends varying amounts by age—adults need 7-9 hours nightly. Sleep deprivation is linked to obesity, high blood pressure, stress, reduced immunity, and impaired cognitive function comparable to alcohol intoxication.
