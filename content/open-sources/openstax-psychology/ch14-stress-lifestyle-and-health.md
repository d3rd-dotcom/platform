### Chapter Introduction

College students today face substantial pressure from multiple sources. Beyond traditional academic demands like exams and papers, contemporary learners contend with "increased college tuitions, burdensome debt, and difficulty finding employment after graduation." Non-traditional students encounter additional challenges such as balancing childcare or full-time employment with their studies.

Stress extends far beyond campus life. People navigate concerns about financial stability, interpersonal conflicts, family obligations, and time management. Even seemingly minor inconveniences—misplaced items, traffic congestion, internet outages—contribute to overall strain and diminish well-being.

Psychologists have studied stress responses and coping mechanisms extensively over nearly a century. This chapter synthesizes current understanding of stress by examining its psychological and physiological dimensions, identifying sources and consequences, and presenting strategies for managing rather than succumbing to stress's effects.

### 14.4 Regulation of Stress (full section content)

**Learning Objectives:** By section's end, students can define coping and distinguish problem-focused from emotion-focused approaches, describe perceived control's importance in stress reactions, and explain social support's role in health and longevity.

**Coping Styles**

Coping refers to "mental and behavioral efforts that we use to deal with problems relating to stress." Lazarus and Folkman (1984) identified two fundamental approaches:

Problem-focused coping involves managing or altering the stressor itself through identifying problems, considering solutions, weighing costs/benefits, and selecting alternatives. Example: A failing student might contact their professor, establish study schedules, and seek tutoring.

Emotion-focused coping consists of efforts to "change or reduce the negative emotions associated with stress" through avoidance, positive comparisons, finding meaning in negative events, or reappraisal. Example: The same student might watch movies or play video games to distract themselves.

Problem-focused coping predominates with controllable stressors, while emotion-focused coping emerges with uncontrollable ones. For uncontrollable situations like bereavement, emotion-focused strategies prove most helpful.

**Control and Stress**

Perceived control—"our beliefs about our personal capacity to exert influence over and shape outcomes"—significantly impacts stress reactions. Albert Bandura stated that "the intensity and chronicity of human stress is governed largely by perceived control over the demands of one's life."

Research demonstrates that higher perceived control correlates with better physical and mental health, greater psychological well-being, and lower emotional/physical reactivity to stressors.

*Learned Helplessness*

Martin Seligman's classic experiments showed dogs subjected to inescapable shocks later failed to escape avoidable shocks, suggesting "learned helplessness"—a belief in one's powerlessness. Seligman theorized this might explain human depression when people experience uncontrollable negative events.

The reformulated model emphasizes attributions (mental explanations) people make about negative events. Students making internal, stable, and global attributions for poor exam performance become especially depression-prone. Attribution habits can improve through training.

Greater perceived control links to lower risks of heart disease, cardiovascular mortality, and physical functioning declines.

**Social Support**

Social support represents "the soothing impact of friends, family, and acquaintances," including advice, encouragement, acceptance, emotional comfort, and tangible assistance.

A landmark Alameda County study following nearly 7,000 residents over nine years found isolated individuals 2.3-2.8 times more likely to die than socially connected peers—effects persisting after controlling for health variables.

A statistical review of 148 studies (1982-2007) involving 300,000+ participants concluded individuals with stronger social relationships have "a 50% greater likelihood of survival" compared to those with weak relationships—an effect magnitude comparable to quitting smoking.

Social support correlates with better survival rates following breast cancer, reduced common cold contraction, and improved immune functioning. It reduces blood pressure during stressful tasks, particularly when support comes from friends.

College students experiencing reduced social support face vulnerability to anxiety, depression, and loneliness, making peer mentoring programs valuable.

**Stress and Discrimination**

Discrimination functions as an uncontrollable, persistent, unpredictable stressor affecting both mental and physical health, particularly for marginalized groups experiencing multiple discrimination forms. The general adaptation syndrome explains how repeated discrimination triggers chronic stress responses, eventually leading to exhaustion and health problems including depression, cardiovascular disease, and cancer.

Protective strategies like social support and healthy coping buffer discrimination's impact, though legal protections and antidiscrimination efforts remain necessary.

**Stress Reduction Techniques**

Exercise benefits both physical and mental health. Physically fit individuals resist stress better and recover faster than less fit individuals. Exercise may buffer physiological stress mechanisms, prevent telomere shortening, and minimize stress effects on memory and the hippocampus.

Relaxation response technique (developed by Herbert Benson in the 1970s) combines relaxation with transcendental meditation through four components: comfortable sitting position, quiet environment with closed eyes, repeating a mantra, and passively focusing on pleasant thoughts. This reduces sympathetic arousal and treats high blood pressure.

Biofeedback (developed by Gary Schwartz) uses electronic equipment measuring neuromuscular and autonomic activity, providing visual or auditory feedback enabling voluntary control over normally involuntary processes. Applications include tension headaches, high blood pressure, asthma, and phobias.
