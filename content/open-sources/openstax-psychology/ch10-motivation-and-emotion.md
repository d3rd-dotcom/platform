### Chapter Introduction

This chapter explores the fundamental forces driving human behavior through examination of motivation and emotion. The opening scenario references the Boston Marathon bombing, using the varied responses—some helping others while some fled—to illustrate how emotions trigger instantaneous behavioral shifts.

Core questions: What compels our actions? What biological mechanisms underlie eating and sexual behavior? How universal are emotional experiences across cultures?

**10.1 Motivation** — Examines theories explaining why individuals engage in particular behaviors, covering both physiological drives and social influences.

**10.2 Hunger and Eating** — Investigates the physiological mechanisms of hunger and satiety, including eating disorder causes and treatments.

**10.3 Sexual Behavior, Sexuality, and Gender Identity** — Traces research evolution regarding human sexuality, sexual orientation, and gender identity understanding.

**10.4 Emotion** — Surveys theories explaining emotional occurrence, biological foundations, and cross-cultural emotional expression patterns.

The chapter synthesizes these topics to provide comprehensive understanding of what motivates human behavior and how we experience and express our emotional lives.

### 10.4 Emotion (full section content)

**Learning Objectives:** The section aims to help students explain major emotion theories, describe limbic structures' roles in emotional processing, and understand the universal nature of emotional expression and recognition.

**Definition and Components of Emotion**

An emotion is described as "a subjective state of being that we often describe as our feelings." Emotions result from combining subjective experience, expression, cognitive appraisal, and physiological responses. The section distinguishes emotions from moods: emotions are intense, conscious responses to specific stimuli, while moods are prolonged, less intense affective states without clear triggers.

**Theories of Emotion**

The text outlines four major theoretical frameworks:

- James-Lange Theory: Emotional experience follows physiological arousal. You feel fear after your body responds to a threat.
- Cannon-Bard Theory: Physiological arousal and emotional experience occur simultaneously and independently.
- Schachter-Singer Two-Factor Theory: Emotions result from physiological arousal interpreted through cognitive context. The famous experiment demonstrated that identical physiological states can produce different emotions depending on cognitive labeling.
- Lazarus's Cognitive-Mediational Theory: "Our emotions are determined by our appraisal of the stimulus," with appraisal preceding emotional response.

The section also discusses the facial feedback hypothesis, suggesting facial expressions can influence emotional intensity, and introduces automatic emotion regulation (AER), which operates without deliberate thought. Additionally, constructivist theory proposes emotions are predictions constructed from experience rather than innate responses.

**Biological Basis: The Limbic System**

The limbic system components include:

- Hypothalamus: Activates sympathetic nervous system responses
- Thalamus: Sensory relay center
- Amygdala: Processes emotional information; basolateral complex handles classical conditioning and emotional value attachment; central nucleus regulates autonomic and endocrine systems
- Hippocampus: Integrates emotional experience with cognition

Research links amygdala and hippocampal dysfunction to mood and anxiety disorders.

**Facial Expressions and Cultural Factors**

The text emphasizes that seven universal facial expressions exist: happiness, surprise, sadness, fright, disgust, contempt, and anger. However, cultural display rules govern emotional expression frequency and appropriateness, varying across cultures. Body language also communicates emotional states through position and movement.
