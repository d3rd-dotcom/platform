### Chapter Introduction

The introduction poses fundamental questions about human thought processes: "What is the best way to solve a problem? How does a person who has never seen or touched snow in real life develop an understanding of" it? These inquiries drive cognitive psychology—the study of thinking.

The field examines intelligence variations across individuals and explores whether practical competence constitutes a distinct form of intelligence. It also investigates what IQ assessments actually measure.

The chapter builds on previously covered cognitive processes like perception, learning, and memory by focusing on "high-level cognitive processes." Readers will explore language development, problem-solving strategies, and creativity. The material concludes by examining how intelligence gets measured and how biological and environmental factors interact to shape cognitive abilities, ultimately deepening appreciation for the sophisticated mental capacities that distinguish humans.

### 7.1 What Is Cognition? (full section content)

**Learning Objectives:** The section outlines four key learning goals: describing cognition, distinguishing concepts and prototypes, explaining natural versus artificial concepts, and describing how schemata are organized and constructed.

**Cognition Definition**

The material defines cognition as "thinking, and it encompasses the processes associated with perception, knowledge, problem solving, judgment, language, and memory." Cognitive psychology examines how people think by studying interactions among thinking, emotion, creativity, language, and problem-solving.

**Concepts and Prototypes**

The brain organizes information through concepts—categories grouping linguistic information, images, ideas, or memories. A prototype is described as "the best example or representation of a concept," such as a Golden Retriever representing the category "dog."

**Natural and Artificial Concepts**

Natural concepts develop through direct or indirect experiences (example: understanding snow through observation or films). Artificial concepts are defined by specific characteristics that remain constant (example: triangles always have three angles and three sides).

**Schemata**

A schema is "a mental construct consisting of a cluster or collection of related concepts." Two types are discussed:

- Role schemas: assumptions about how people in specific roles behave
- Event schemas/cognitive scripts: routine behavioral sets (example: elevator etiquette)

The text emphasizes that while schemata enable efficient information processing, they can lead to inaccurate assumptions about individuals.
