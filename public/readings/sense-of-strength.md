
This week tackles another major creative block: time. You will explore the ways in which you have used your perception of time to preclude taking creative risks. You will identify immediate and practical changes you can make in your current life. You will excavate the early conditioning that may have encouraged you to settle for far less than you desire creatively.

## Survival

One of the most difficult tasks an artist must face is artistic survival. All artists must learn the art of surviving loss: loss of hope, loss of face, loss of money, loss of self-belief. These losses are hazards of the road and, in many ways, its signposts.

Artistic losses become scar tissue when they are not openly acknowledged or mourned. The book that does not sell, the film that is not picked up, the juried show that does not take our work, the injury that sidelines us, the role we did not get, the canceled show, the cruel review, the abandoned project: these are all losses, and they must be mourned.

The artist within, like the child within, is seldom hurt by truth. What damages us is criticism that contains no saving kernel of truth yet lands with a certain damning plausibility. Teachers, mentors, and authority figures can wound artists deeply when they shame the work or the dream itself.

## The Ivory Power

Many intellectual environments are not friendly to creativity. Skilled in analysis, they often know how to take things apart but not how to nurture something into being. Younger artists are seedlings. Early work resembles underbrush, not polished theory. When artists are shamed for not conforming to norms that are not truly theirs, audacity can get pinched out long before talent does.

To recover our hope and courage, we must acknowledge and mourn the scars that are blocking us. That includes wounds inflicted by teachers, institutions, and our own fear.

## Gain Disguised as Loss

Every loss can be framed as a potential gain. The useful question is not "Why me?" but "How can this loss serve me? Where does it point my work?"

Whenever one avenue is blocked, another can open. The task is to metabolize pain as energy. The key to career resiliency is self-empowerment and choice. Rather than let loss harden into bitterness, we look for the other door.

When faced with a loss, immediately take one small action to support your artist. Buy flowers. Buy a sketchpad. Make a promise. Show up for the work again.

## Age and Time: Product and Process

"I'm too old" is one of the great block lies. It is usually an evasive tactic used to avoid facing fear. The real answer to "How old will I be by the time I learn to do that?" is: the same age you will be if you do not.

Creativity occurs in process, not product. "I am writing a screenplay" is more alive than "I wrote a screenplay." The same is true of acting, painting, music, and any other form. Creativity lies in the doing, not just in the done.

The willingness to be a beginner is one of the strongest tools an artist has. Beginner's humility and openness lead to exploration. Exploration leads to accomplishment.

## Filling the Form

Filling the form means taking the next small step instead of skipping ahead to a dramatic leap for which you are not yet prepared. If you want to sell a screenplay, first write one. If you want to paint, first paint. If you want to act, first take the class, get the headshot, do the workshop, learn the craft.

Blocked creatives often fantasize about changing their whole lives in one sweeping move. That drama lets them avoid the next small action. Creativity requires activity, and the activity is usually modest:

- washing the paintbrushes
- checking the paper for acting classes
- clearing a corner to work in
- writing today's pages
- making one phone call

The big questions often function as procrastination. What matters is the next right thing.

## Affirmations

Select five affirmations to work with this week:

- I am a talented person.
- I have a right to be an artist.
- I am a good person and a good artist.
- Creativity is a blessing I accept.
- My creativity blesses others.
- My creativity is appreciated.
- I now treat myself and my creativity more gently.
- I now accept hope.
- I now act affirmatively.
- I now allow myself to heal.

## Tasks

1. Name your dream. Name one concrete goal that signals its accomplishment. Then name actions for five years, one year, one month, one week, and now.
2. Write a page about the childhood you might have had with perfect nurturing. How can you reparent yourself in that direction now?
3. Pick a color and describe yourself in the first person as that color.
4. List five things you are not allowed to do. Then do one of them on paper: write it, draw it, paint it, act it out, or dance it.
5. List twenty things you like to do, then note whether each is free or costly, solitary or shared, risky or safe, fast or slow, mind or body or spirit.
6. Plan a perfect day in your life as it is now.
7. Plan an ideal perfect day in your life as you wish it were.
8. Choose one festive aspect from your ideal day and let yourself live it.
