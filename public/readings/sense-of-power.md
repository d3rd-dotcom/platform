## Anger

Anger is fuel. We feel it and want to do something. Hit someone, break something, throw a fit, smash a fist into the wall, tell those bastards. But most of us do everything except listen to our anger. We stuff it, deny it, bury it, block it, medicate it, muffle it, or ignore it.

Anger is meant to be listened to. It is a voice, a shout, a plea, a demand. Anger is a map. It shows us what our boundaries are, where we want to go, and where we have been without liking it. Anger points the way, not just the finger. In the recovery of a blocked artist, anger is a sign of health.

Anger is meant to be acted upon, not acted out. It points the direction. We are meant to use anger as fuel to take the actions we need to move where our anger points us.

I merely took the energy it takes to pout and wrote some blues.  
DUKE ELLINGTON

"Blast him! I could make a better film than that!" This anger says: you want to make movies. You need to learn how.

"I can't believe it! I had this idea for a play three years ago, and she's gone and written it." This anger says: stop procrastinating. Ideas do not get opening nights. Finished plays do.

"That's my strategy he's using. This is incredible. I've been ripped off." This anger says: take your own ideas seriously enough to treat them well.

Anger tells us the old life is dying. It tells us we are being reborn, and birthing hurts. The hurt makes us angry. Anger is the firestorm that signals the death of our old life and the fuel that propels us into our new one.

Sloth, apathy, and despair are the enemy. Anger is not. Anger is our friend. Not a nice friend. Not a gentle friend. But a very loyal friend. It will always tell us when we have been betrayed. It will always tell us when we have betrayed ourselves. It will always tell us that it is time to act in our own best interests.

Anger is not the action itself. It is action's invitation.

## Useful Timing

Useful openings can be scary because they create responsibility. You asked for a chance. Now that a chance is here, what are you going to do with it?

Creative recovery often makes useful timing visible:

- A woman admits to a buried dream of acting and sits beside a beginning-actors teacher the very next night.
- A writer makes one exploratory call to film school and discovers the last available slot is now his.
- A woman thinks about going back to school and opens the mail to find a letter from that exact school.
- A businessman who has secretly written for years meets a professional writer who becomes his mentor and collaborator.

Life is what we make of it. The useful question is not whether support is internal or external. The useful question is what happens when you ask, experiment, and record the results. Take a small step toward a dream and watch which doors open.

Useful opportunities often appear after you take visible risks.
SHAKTI GAWAIN

Whatever you think you can do or believe you can do, begin it. Action has momentum and power in it.
GOETHE

## Shame

Many of us are sabotaged not only by fear but by shame. Shame is a controlling device. Making a piece of art can feel like telling a family secret. It asks, "What will they think of me once they know this?"

Art opens the closets, airs out the cellars and attics, and brings healing. But before a wound can heal it must be seen, and that act of exposure is often reacted to with shaming. Bad reviews are a prime source of shame for many artists. Childhood shaming can make even beginning or finishing a piece of art feel dangerous.

Not all criticism is shaming. Accurate criticism often brings an inner sense of relief: "Ah, so that's what was wrong with it." The criticism that damages is that which disparages, dismisses, ridicules, or condemns. It is often vague and hard to refute.

Because of this, artists must learn to be very self-protective. A first draft is seldom appropriately shown to anyone but the most gentle and discerning eye. It often takes another artist to see the embryonic work that is trying to sprout.

## Dealing With Criticism

1. Receive the criticism all the way through and get it over with.
2. Jot down what concepts or phrases bother you.
3. Jot down what concepts or phrases seem useful.
4. Do something nurturing for yourself. Read an old good review or recall a compliment.
5. Remember that even a truly rotten piece of art may be a necessary stepping-stone to your next work.
6. Notice whether the criticism triggers older, shaming criticism from your past.
7. Write a letter to the critic, probably not to be mailed. Defend your work and acknowledge what was helpful.
8. Get back on the horse. Make an immediate commitment to do something creative.
9. Do it. Creativity is the only cure for criticism.

Artists who seek perfection in everything are those who cannot attain it in anything.  
EUGENE DELACROIX

## Growth

Growth is an erratic forward movement: two steps forward, one step back. Remember that and be gentle with yourself. A creative recovery is a healing process. You are capable of great things on Tuesday, but on Wednesday you may slide backward. This is normal.

Very often, a week of insights will be followed by a week of sluggishness. The field notes will seem pointless. They are not. What you are learning to do is rest on the page. If you write three pages every morning and do one kind thing for yourself every day, you will begin to notice a slight lightness of heart.

Practice being kind to yourself in small, concrete ways. Look at your refrigerator. Are you feeding yourself nicely? Do you have socks? An extra set of sheets? A new house plant? A thermos for the long drive to work? Allow yourself to pitch out some old ragged clothes. You do not have to keep everything.

There is a vitality, a life force, an energy, a quickening, that is translated through you into action, and because there is only one of you in all time, this expression is unique.  
MARTHA GRAHAM

## Tasks

1. Describe your childhood room. If you wish, sketch it. What was your favorite thing about it? What is your favorite thing about your room right now?
2. Describe five traits you like in yourself as a child.
3. List five childhood accomplishments and five favorite childhood foods. Buy yourself one of those foods this week.
4. List three obvious rotten habits and three subtle forms of sabotage. What payoff do you get from continuing them?
5. List three nurturing friends. What traits in them serve you well?
6. Call a friend who treats you like a bright and capable person.
7. Follow your inner compass for an hour by doing an artist-brain activity and listening to the insights that arise.
8. List five people you admire and five you secretly admire. What traits do they have that you can cultivate in yourself?
9. List five people you wish you had met and five dead people you would like to spend time with in eternity. What traits do you seek in them?
10. Compare the lists. Notice what you truly admire versus what you think you should admire.
