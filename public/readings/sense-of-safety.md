### Shadow Artists

[cite_start]One of our chief needs as creative beings is support. [cite: 5, 6] [cite_start]Ideally, we would be nurtured and encouraged first by our nuclear family and then by ever-widening circles of friends and teachers. [cite: 7] [cite_start]As young artists, we want to be acknowledged for our attempts and efforts as well as our achievements. [cite: 8] [cite_start]Unfortunately, many artists never receive this critical early encouragement and may not even know they are artists at all. [cite: 9]

[cite_start]Parents often offer cautionary advice instead of support. [cite: 11] [cite_start]Timid young artists, adding parental fears to their own, often give up their dreams and settle into a world of regrets. [cite: 12] [cite_start]There, caught between the dream of action and the fear of failure, **shadow artists** are born. [cite: 13] 

#### Case Examples:
* [cite_start]**Edwin**: A wealthy trader who is miserable because he was urged into finance despite being gifted in visual arts. [cite: 14, 15] [cite_start]He surrounds himself with artifacts but believes creativity is the prerogative of others. [cite: 21, 22]
* [cite_start]**Erin**: A children's therapist who suppressed her creative urges for two decades. [cite: 33, 37] [cite_start]She eventually realized she was pouring all her creativity into helping others while abandoning her own "artist child." [cite: 35, 37]
* [cite_start]**Jerry**: A man who was afraid to pursue filmmaking, so he instead poured his energy into his girlfriend's art career. [cite: 48, 51]

[cite_start]Shadow artists often choose **shadow careers**—jobs that are close to the desired art but are not the art itself. [cite: 57] Examples include:
* [cite_start]Fiction writers becoming journalists or advertisers. [cite: 59]
* [cite_start]Artists becoming artist managers. [cite: 60]
* [cite_start]Gifted photographers working as photographer reps. [cite: 65]

### Protecting the Artist Child Within

[cite_start]Your "artist" is a child that must be found and protected. [cite: 91] [cite_start]Learning to create is like learning to walk; you must start by crawling. [cite: 92] [cite_start]This involves taking "baby steps" and accepting that there will be falls—such as bad first paintings or poems. [cite: 102, 93]

[cite_start]Recovering shadow artists often engage in "artist abuse" by measuring beginning work against masterworks or exposing it to premature criticism. [cite: 94, 95, 96] To recover, you must:
* [cite_start]**Go gently and slowly**: Healing old wounds is the goal. [cite: 100, 101]
* [cite_start]**Aim for progress, not perfection**: You cannot get better and look good at the same time. [cite: 103, 108]
* [cite_start]**Be willing to be a bad artist**: Giving yourself permission to be a beginner gives you the chance to eventually be a very good artist. [cite: 112, 113]

### Your Enemy Within: Core Negative Beliefs

[cite_start]Most blocks exist because we feel safer being unhappy than facing the unknown of our own creativity. [cite: 117, 119] We often hold unconscious negative beliefs, such as:
* [cite_start]"Everyone will hate me." [cite: 126]
* [cite_start]"I will never have any real money." [cite: 138]
* [cite_start]"It's too late." [cite: 145]

[cite_start]These beliefs are not facts; they are acquired from parents, teachers, and culture. [cite: 146, 150] [cite_start]They often present "either/or" reasoning, such as "I can either be financially successful or an artist," when it is actually possible to be both. [cite: 164, 167]

### Your Ally Within: Affirmative Weapons

[cite_start]Affirmations are positive statements of belief that help you allow yourself to create. [cite: 176] [cite_start]When you write an affirmation (e.g., "I am a brilliant and prolific painter"), your "Censor" will likely object with **blurts**. [cite: 180, 182, 183] 

[cite_start]Blurts are the ugly, specific objections that pop up, like "Who do you think you are?" [cite: 184] [cite_start]You must listen to these blurts, identify where they came from (like a malevolent teacher or parent), and then convert them into positive affirmations to dissolve them. [cite: 190, 205, 206]

### Tasks for Week 1

1.  [cite_start]**Morning Pages**: Every morning, write three pages of longhand freewriting. [cite: 208] [cite_start]Do not reread them or let anyone else see them. [cite: 209]
2.  [cite_start]**Artist Date**: Once a week, take yourself on a solo expedition to do something fun or silly, like visiting a five-and-dime store to buy gold stars or stickers. [cite: 215, 217, 218]
3.  [cite_start]**Time Travel**: Write out a "horror story" from your past involving someone who discouraged your creativity (a "monster"). [cite: 219] [cite_start]Sketch the monster or draw a red X through it. [cite: 222, 223]
4.  [cite_start]**Letter in Defense**: Write a letter to the "editor" (or yourself) in the voice of your wounded artist child, defending your right to create. [cite: 224, 225]
5.  [cite_start]**Imaginary Lives**: Jot down other lives you might have liked to lead (e.g., country singer, cowhand) and try to do one small thing related to one of those lives this week. [cite: 228, 231]
6.  [cite_start]**Artist Walk**: Take a brisk twenty-minute walk to help shift your attention. [cite: 236, 237]
