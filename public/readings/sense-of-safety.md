This week initiates your creative recovery. You may feel both giddy and defiant, hopeful and skeptical. The readings, tasks, and exercises aim at allowing you to establish a sense of safety, which will enable you to explore your creativity with less fear.

---

### Shadow Artists

One of our chief needs as creative beings is support. Ideally, we would be nurtured and encouraged first by our nuclear family and then by ever-widening circles of friends and teachers. As young artists, we want to be acknowledged for our attempts and efforts as well as our achievements. Unfortunately, many artists never receive this critical early encouragement and may not even know they are artists at all.

Parents often offer cautionary advice instead of support. Timid young artists, adding parental fears to their own, often give up their dreams and settle into a world of regrets. There, caught between the dream of action and the fear of failure, **shadow artists** are born. 

#### Case Examples:
* **Edwin**: A wealthy trader who is miserable because he was urged into finance despite being gifted in visual arts. He surrounds himself with artifacts but believes creativity is the prerogative of others.
* **Erin**: A children's therapist who suppressed her creative urges for two decades. She eventually realized she was pouring all her creativity into helping others while abandoning her own "artist child."
* **Jerry**: A man who was afraid to pursue filmmaking, so he instead poured his energy into his girlfriend's art career.

Shadow artists often choose **shadow careers**—jobs that are close to the desired art but are not the art itself. Examples include:
* Fiction writers becoming journalists or advertisers.
* Artists becoming artist managers.
* Gifted photographers working as photographer reps.

### Protecting the Artist Child Within

Your "artist" is a child that must be found and protected. Learning to create is like learning to walk; you must start by crawling. This involves taking "baby steps" and accepting that there will be falls—such as bad first paintings or poems.

Recovering shadow artists often engage in "artist abuse" by measuring beginning work against masterworks or exposing it to premature criticism. To recover, you must:
* **Go gently and slowly**: Healing old wounds is the goal.
* **Aim for progress, not perfection**: You cannot get better and look good at the same time.
* **Be willing to be a bad artist**: Giving yourself permission to be a beginner gives you the chance to eventually be a very good artist.

### Your Enemy Within: Core Negative Beliefs

Most blocks exist because we feel safer being unhappy than facing the unknown of our own creativity. We often hold unconscious negative beliefs, such as:
* "Everyone will hate me."
* "I will never have any real money."
* "It's too late."

These beliefs are not facts; they are acquired from parents, teachers, and culture. They often present "either/or" reasoning, such as "I can either be financially successful or an artist," when it is actually possible to be both.

---

### Your Ally Within: Affirmative Weapons

Affirmations are positive statements of belief that help you allow yourself to create. When you write an affirmation (e.g., "I am a brilliant and prolific painter"), your "Censor" will likely object with **blurts**. 

Blurts are the ugly, specific objections that pop up, like "Who do you think you are?" You must listen to these blurts, identify where they came from (like a malevolent teacher or parent), and then convert them into positive affirmations to dissolve them.

### Tasks for Week 1

1.  **Field Notes**: Every morning, write three pages of longhand freewriting. Do not reread them or let anyone else see them.
2.  **Artist Date**: Once a week, take yourself on a solo expedition to do something fun or silly, like visiting a five-and-dime store to buy gold stars or stickers.
3.  **Time Travel**: Write out a "horror story" from your past involving someone who discouraged your creativity (a "monster"). Sketch the monster or draw a red X through it.
4.  **Letter in Defense**: Write a letter to the "editor" (or yourself) in the voice of your wounded artist child, defending your right to create.
5.  **Imaginary Lives**: Jot down other lives you might have liked to lead (e.g., country singer, cowhand) and try to do one small thing related to one of those lives this week.
6.  **Artist Walk**: Take a brisk twenty-minute walk to help shift your attention.

### Check-In

At the end of the week, answer these questions by hand:
* How many days did you do your field notes?
* Did you do your artist date? What did you do and how did it feel?
* Were there any other significant issues this week?
