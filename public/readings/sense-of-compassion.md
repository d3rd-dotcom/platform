This week finds us facing the internal blocks to creativity. It may be tempting to abandon ship at this point. Do not. We will explore and acknowledge the emotional difficulties that beset us in the past as we made creative efforts. We will undertake healing the shame of past failures. We will gain in compassion as we reparent the frightened artist child who yearns for creative accomplishment.

## Fear

One of the most important tasks in artistic recovery is learning to call things by the right names. Most of us have spent years using the wrong names for our behavior. We have wanted to create, been unable to create, and called that inability laziness. That is not merely inaccurate. It is cruel.

Blocked artists are not lazy. They are blocked.

The blocked artist expends a great deal of energy, just not visibly. That energy goes into self-hatred, regret, grief, jealousy, self-doubt, and fear. The blocked artist thinks in terms of great big scary impossible tasks. When those tasks are not accomplished, the blocked artist calls that laziness.

Do not call the inability to start laziness. Call it fear.

Fear is the true name for what ails the blocked artist. It may be fear of failure or fear of success. Often it is fear of abandonment. That fear has roots in childhood reality. Many artists tried to become artists against either their parents' good wishes or their parents' good judgment. For a youngster, that is a terrible conflict.

The need to be a great artist makes it hard to be an artist at all. Finding it hard to begin does not mean you will not be able to do it. It means you will need help from supportive friends, practical structure, and yourself. You must give yourself permission to begin small and go in baby steps.

Use love for your artist to cure its fear. Stop yelling at yourself. Be nice. Call fear by its right name.

## Enthusiasm

It is tempting to think art requires discipline in the heroic, Spartan sense. In the short run, discipline may work. In the long run, what sustains the artist is enthusiasm.

Enthusiasm is not just an emotion. It is a practical commitment to the creative process and a willingness to notice what gives you energy. True enthusiasm is grounded in play, not grim duty.

Our artist is our child within, our playmate. It works best when treated accordingly. Paint is great gooey stuff. Sharpened pencils are fun. Work spaces often function best when they also feel like play spaces.

Art is process. The process is supposed to be fun.

## Creative U-Turns

Recovering from artist's block requires relinquishing the mixed privileges of emotional invalidism. A productive artist is often a happy person, and that can feel threatening if we are used to getting our needs met through unhappiness.

Many recovering artists sabotage themselves right before or right after a creative victory. They make creative U-turns:

- they do not make the requested revisions
- they do not follow up with the interested agent
- they stop performing after one painful reception
- they shelve the project after a promising deal
- they pick a fight right when a door opens

These U-turns are born from fear. They deserve compassion, not more shame.

To recover from a creative U-turn, admit it exists. Ask for help. Find someone who has survived the jump you are afraid of. Let other artists teach you how they handled agents, rewrites, reviews, and fear.

## Blasting Through Blocks

Before beginning or restarting a project, ask your artist:

1. What resentments do I have in connection with this project?
2. What fears do I have about the work or the people around it?
3. What tiny fear or tiny anger am I still minimizing?
4. What do I stand to gain by not doing this work?
5. What deal am I willing to make with the creative force?

The deal is simple: "You take care of the quality, I will take care of the quantity."

## Tasks

2. Create an ideal-scene visualization of yourself doing your goal at the height of your powers.

5. Choose an artist totem, something you feel protective toward, and give it a place of honor.
