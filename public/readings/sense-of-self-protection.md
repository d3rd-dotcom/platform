
This week we explore the patterns that can ambush creative progress. In the essays, tasks, and exercises of this week, we search out the habits we cling to when change makes us anxious.

## Dangers of the Trail

Creativity depends on attention, time, and emotional bandwidth. When we are clear about who we are and what we are doing, the work moves more freely. When we resist what the work might show us or where it might take us, we often feel shaky and try to shut the process down.

Every creative person has preferred ways of blocking:

- food
- alcohol
- drugs
- work
- painful love obsession
- sex
- busyness

These are not bad in themselves. The issue is abuse. We reach for them to block anxiety and change. We block because possibility scares us. Unblocked, we may be happy, and happy can feel more dangerous than familiar misery.

## Workaholism

Workaholism is an addiction, and like all addictions, it blocks creative attention. If people are too busy to write field notes or too busy to take an artist date, they are probably too busy to hear what they want to make.

Play can make a workaholic very nervous. Fun is scary.

To see workaholism clearly, ask:

- Do I work outside office hours?
- Do I cancel dates with loved ones to do more work?
- Do I take work home on weekends or vacations?
- Do my intimates complain that I always work?
- Do I prioritize my day to include creative work or play?
- Do I allow myself downtime?
- Do I use the word deadline to rationalize an unreasonable workload?

In order to recover our creativity, we must see workaholism as a block, not a building block.

## Drought

In any creative life there are dry seasons. Life loses sweetness. Work feels mechanical, empty, or forced. We feel we have nothing to say and are tempted to say nothing.

During a drought, field notes seem both painful and foolish. They feel like empty gestures. Yet they are most valuable then. Droughts are terrible and necessary. They bring clarity and compassion. Droughts end because we keep showing up.

To write is to right things. Sooner or later, our pages bring things right.

## Fame

Fame encourages us to believe that if it has not happened yet, it never will. But fame is not the same as success. Success can be felt at the end of a good day's work. Fame is an external metric that can distort the question. It makes us ask, "How am I doing?" instead of, "Is the work going well?"

Fame creates a continual feeling of lack. There is never enough of it. The antidote is self-approval, gentleness, and actual creative work. When the fame drug hits, go to the tools of your craft and do a little creative play.

## Competition

Competition is another external metric that can distort the work. When we focus on others, we poison our own well. Instead of asking whether we worked on our play today or mailed the thing that needed mailing, we compare ourselves, envy, resent, and stall.

No piece of art is born in total isolation from other work. Originality is not the absence of influence. It is the accurate mapping of our own experience and interests.

Never judge fledgling work too quickly. Art needs time to sprawl, incubate, and emerge as itself.

## Tasks

1. Do the Deadlies exercise with seven slips: alcohol, drugs, sex, work, money, food, family or friends. Write five ways each has negatively affected your life.
2. Make a list of touchstones: small things you love that console and strengthen you.
3. Tell the awful truth about which habit blocks you, what payoff you get from it, and which friends strengthen or weaken your belief in yourself.
4. Set a bottom line with five painful behaviors you will no longer allow.
5. Cherish your artist: list five small victories, three nurturing actions you took, three more you can take, and make three promises you will keep.
