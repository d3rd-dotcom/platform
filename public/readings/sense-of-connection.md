This week turns to the practice of right attitudes for creativity. The emphasis is on your receptive as well as active skills. The essays, exercises, and tasks are meant to excavate areas of genuine creative interest as you reconnect with your personal dreams.

The ability to listen is a skill we are honing with both our morning pages and our artist dates. The pages train us to hear past our Censor. The artist dates help us pick up the voice of inspiration. While both of these activities may appear unrelated to the actual act of making art, they are critical to the creative process.

Art is not about thinking something up. It is about the opposite: getting something down.

If we are trying to think something up, we are straining to reach for something just beyond our grasp, as though art lives somewhere high above us in the stratosphere. When we get something down, there is no strain. We are not forcing. Someone or something else is doing the doing. Instead of reaching for inventions, we are engaged in listening.

When an actor is in the moment, he or she is listening for the next right creative move. When a painter paints, there may be a plan at first, but that plan is quickly surrendered to the painting's own plan. Painters often say, "The brush takes the next stroke." In dance, in composition, in sculpture, the experience is the same: we are more conduit than creator.

"Art is an act of tuning in and dropping down the well." It can feel as though stories, paintings, music, and performances live just under the surface of ordinary attention. Like an underground river, they flow through us as a stream of ideas we can tap into. As artists, we drop down the well into that stream. We hear what is down there and act on it.

Most artists have had the experience of catching a paragraph, a poem, a melody, or an image that arrives already formed. We call these moments useful exchanges. What we fail to realize is that they are, in fact, the norm. We are often the instrument more than the author of our work.

Michelangelo is said to have remarked that he released David from the rock in which he found him. Jackson Pollock said, "The painting has a life of its own. I try to let it come through." The same may be said of all art. Paintings wait for us. Sculptures wait for us. Sonatas, books, plays, poems, and films wait for us too. Our job is simply to get them down.

Some people find it easier to picture the stream of inspiration as radio waves being broadcast at all times. With practice, we learn how to hear the desired frequency on request. We tune in. Like a parent, we learn to hear the voice of our current brainchild among all the other voices.

Listening is a form of accepting. Once you accept that it is natural to create, you can begin to accept a second idea: the project may already have more support available than you assumed. The minute you become willing to notice help, you begin to see useful bits of it everywhere in your life.

You may hear the dialogue you need, find the right song for a scene, notice the exact paint color you had almost imagined, or discover a book, seminar, or discarded object that fits what you are working on. Learn to accept useful help when it appears.

Many of us harbor the fear that our creations are decadent, frivolous, or unimportant. Because of this, we discount help. Artists like other artists. Let support count when it arrives.


## Perfectionism

Tillie Olsen called it "the knife of the perfectionist attitude in art." You may call it getting it right, fixing things before moving on, or having standards. What you should call it is perfectionism.

Perfectionism has nothing to do with getting it right. It has nothing to do with fixing things. It has nothing to do with standards. Perfectionism is a refusal to let yourself move ahead. It is a loop, an obsessive closed system that causes you to get stuck in the details of what you are writing or painting or making and lose sight of the whole.

Instead of creating freely and allowing errors to reveal themselves later as insight, we often get mired in getting the details right. We correct our originality into a uniformity that lacks passion and spontaneity. Miles Davis said it plainly: "Do not fear mistakes. There are none."

The perfectionist fixes one line of a poem over and over until no line is right. The perfectionist redraws the chin on a portrait until the paper tears. The perfectionist writes so many versions of scene one that she never reaches the rest of the play. Instead of enjoying the process, the perfectionist grades the results while creating them.

The perfectionist has married the logic side of the brain. The critic reigns supreme in the perfectionist's creative household. A brilliant descriptive passage is interrupted by a white-glove critique: "What about this comma? Is that how you spell this?" For the perfectionist, there are no first drafts, rough sketches, or warm-up exercises. Every draft is expected to be final.

Midway through a project, the perfectionist stops to reread it all, outline it, and ask where it is going. And where is it going? Nowhere, very fast.

The perfectionist is never satisfied. There is always room for improvement. The perfectionist may call this humility. In reality, it is egotism. It is pride that makes us want to write a perfect script, paint a perfect painting, or perform a perfect audition monologue.

Perfectionism is not a quest for the best. It is a pursuit of the worst in ourselves, the part that tells us nothing we do will ever be good enough and that we should start over again.

No. We should not.

Paul Gardner said, "A painting is never finished. It simply stops in interesting places." A book is never finished either. At a certain point, you stop writing it and go on to the next thing. A film is never cut perfectly, but at a certain point you let go and call it done. That is a normal part of creativity. We always do the best we can by the light we have to see by.


## Risk

Question: What would I do if I did not have to do it perfectly?

We have all heard that the unexamined life is not worth living, but consider too that the unlived life is not worth examining. The success of a creative recovery hinges on our ability to move out of the head and into action. This brings us squarely to risk.

Most of us are practiced at talking ourselves out of risk. We are skilled speculators on the probable pain of self-exposure. "I'll look like an idiot," we say, summoning memories of our first acting class, our first hobbled short story, our terrible drawings. We line up the masters and compare our baby steps against their perfected craft. We compare our student films to *Star Wars* instead of comparing them to George Lucas's student films.

We deny that in order to do something well we must first be willing to do it badly. Instead, we set our limits where success feels guaranteed. Living within these limits, we may feel stifled, smothered, despairing, or bored. But yes, we do feel safe. And safety is a very expensive illusion.

In order to risk, we must jettison our accepted limits. We must break through "I can't because..." Because I am too old, too broke, too shy, too proud? Usually, when we say we cannot do something, what we mean is that we will not do it unless we can guarantee doing it perfectly.

Working artists know the folly of this stance. There is a common joke among directors: "I always know exactly how I should have directed the picture after I'm done directing it." Artists who look successful from the outside often feel blocked on the inside because they are afraid to move into new and more satisfying territory. They become typecast. Singers stay married to safe material. Songwriters try to repeat a formula. They stop risking growth.

Once we are willing to accept that anything worth doing may even be worth doing badly, our options widen. "If I didn't have to do it perfectly, I would try..."

1. Stand-up comedy.
2. Modern dancing.
3. Whitewater rafting.
4. Archery.
5. Learning German.
6. Figure drawing.
7. Figure skating.
8. Being a platinum blond.
9. Puppeteering.
10. Trapeze.
11. Water ballet.
12. Polo.
13. Wearing red lipstick.
14. Taking a couture class.
15. Writing stories.
16. Reading my poetry in public.
17. A spontaneous tropical vacation.
18. Learning to shoot video.
19. Learning to ride a bike.
20. Taking a watercolor class.

There is no must in art because art is free. A risk is often worth taking simply for the sake of taking it. There is something enlivening about expanding our self-definition, and a risk does exactly that. Selecting a challenge and meeting it creates a sense of self-empowerment that becomes the ground for further successful challenges.

Complete the sentence: "If I didn't have to do it perfectly, I would try..."


## Jealousy

Jealousy is often dismissed as a normal human emotion, but creative jealousy deserves closer attention. It roars in the head, tightens the chest, and grips the stomach. It feels ugly. Yet it can also be a tough-love friend.

Jealousy is a map.

Each of our jealousy maps is different. You may be surprised by what yours reveals. For years, I was not especially threatened by the success of women novelists, but I took an unhealthy interest in the fortunes and misfortunes of women playwrights. I was their harshest critic until I wrote my first play. With that action, my jealousy vanished, replaced by camaraderie. My jealousy had actually been pointing toward something I wanted deeply but had not yet been brave enough to pursue.

Jealousy is always a mask for fear: fear that we are unable to get what we want, fear that somebody else is getting what is rightfully ours, fear that we are too frightened to reach for it ourselves. At its root, jealousy is a stingy emotion. It does not allow for abundance. It says there is room for only one poet, one painter, one performer, one whatever you dream of becoming.

The truth revealed by action in the direction of our dreams is that there is room for all of us.

But jealousy produces tunnel vision. It strips us of perspective. It narrows our ability to see options. And its biggest lie is that we have no choice but to stay jealous. In reality, action is the antidote. If jealousy points to a dream, then the answer is not to brood over the person who is doing it. The answer is to begin doing it yourself.

With courage, we dare to take risks. With courage, we make room for compassion and humility. Courage is the foundation of integrity, and action is the way out of envy. What jealousy identifies, action can reclaim.
