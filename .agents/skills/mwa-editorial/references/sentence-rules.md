# Sentence-Level Rules

Line-level mechanics from `EDITORIAL.md` Part 3. Apply on every pass.

## Cut "that" constructions

- No: "The platform that we built that allows users to..."
- Yes: "Our platform allows users to..."

## Replace abstractions with specifics

- No: "communal rewards"
- Yes: "Experiments and courses earn Diamonds"

If you can't replace an abstraction with a number, a name, a date, a verb, or a noun, the abstraction is doing nothing — cut it.

## Delete hedge words

Cut on sight: *kind of, sort of, maybe, possibly, somewhat, a bit, fairly, pretty, just, simply, basically, essentially, really, very, quite*.

- No: "We kind of offer something fresh"
- Yes: "We offer something fresh"

If the claim is too strong without the hedge, the claim itself is wrong — make a smaller, true claim.

## One idea per sentence

- No: "We skip forceful subscriptions and instead offer meaningful experiences, fresh perspectives, and interactive characters that blend into the app."
- Yes: "No subscriptions. Earn rewards. Rely on an agentic system to keep it transparent."

Short sentences carry weight. Use periods.

## Specifics over generics

| Generic | Specific |
|---|---|
| "users" | "members" or "participants" |
| "rewards" | name the actual reward: "credits", "Diamonds", "a cash bounty" — never a ticker or chain name |
| "the platform" | name the actual surface (the gameworld, the quest board, the courses page) |
| "AI" | "Blue" (when it's her) or "an AI agent with memory" |

## Technology stays backstage (hard rule)

Marketing and outward copy never name the underlying technology. Banned in marketing: *Base, $BLUE, blockchain, onchain, crypto, token, wallet, mint, burn, gas, NFT, DEX, smart contract, transaction hash*, and any ticker (including USDC — say "cash" or "a dollar bounty").

Say what the reader gets: rewards that are real, permanent, and theirs. If a sentence needs a chain term to land, it belongs in `docs/tokenomics/` or a technical surface, not in copy. In the rare technical surface where the mechanics ARE the content, "onchain" is one word.

## Numbers

Use a real, current number when it changes the reader's understanding. Pull it from the live product or a source you can verify today. Never bake stats into reusable copy or templates, and never invent one — stale numbers read as lies the day they expire.

## Active voice, present tense

- No: "Submissions are reviewed by Blue"
- Yes: "Blue reviews submissions."

## Capitalization

Title Case is fine for feature titles and section headers. Never set words or headings in all caps — including inside prompt examples. Her name is written **Blue** in user-facing copy; `BLUE` appears only in code and contract identifiers, and never in marketing.

## The cut test

After a draft, count what you cut. If the second draft isn't roughly 30% shorter than the first, you proofread instead of editing. Go again.
