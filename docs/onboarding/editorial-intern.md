## Day one: memorize three things

**1. What we are (30 seconds).**

> Mental Wealth Academy is a social-first gameworld for learning, research, and rewards. A gameworld built around behavioral psychology, custom science tools, and Blue, our AI companion — with permanent rewards, real ownership, and anonymous-by-default participation.

**2. The core pillars.**

1. **Education** — accelerate quality education: sensible, free to all, and rewarding to learn.
2. **Environment** — mental wealth is our social ability to redefine prosperity beyond material assets.
3. **Evolution** — invest in human potential; compounding virtuous actions lead to a better future.

Tagline: *"Investing in the future of humanity, with the heart of tomorrow."* Grounded meaning: small, consistent actions today make the future measurably better.

**3. Who you are writing for.** Pick the reader before you write.

- **Primary** — Gen-Z individuals seeking structured growth, ages 18 and up. Grew up online, drawn to psychology and self-improvement, motivated by XP and rewards, want accountability and real stakes, thrive on quick well-scoped tasks.
- **Secondary** — scientists and academics, including citizen scientists and special-interest groups. They want collaborative experimentation and research tied to live participants.

Most consumer surfaces speak to the Primary reader. Never mix both audiences inside one section. If a surface needs both, separate them physically.

## The one test

Before writing or accepting any sentence, ask:

> **Does this communicate an educational gameworld, an academic aspect, or a feature — meaningfully?**

If it doesn't land on one of those three concretely, cut it or ground it. This single question catches more drift than every other rule combined. Make it a reflex.

## The hierarchy of information

Every concept introduction follows this order:

1. **What it is** — functional definition
2. **What it does** — practical reality
3. **Why it matters** — value proposition
4. **What it means** — narrative or philosophical layer

Most drafts jump straight to step four without earning steps one through three. Reorder before publishing.

---

## Ground then elevate

The central rule. Every poetic claim is preceded by its concrete meaning: **concrete fact, then narrative framing.** The story layer earns its place by attaching to something real and verifiable. Without the anchor, copy reads inflated.

Ungrounded:

> "A new awakening engine guides the self."

Grounded, then elevated:

> "Blue is an AI agent with memory. She reviews platform data and submissions, and pays rewards herself. Her characterization makes her feel more like infrastructure than a feature — the mascot of the Academy and a champion for your mental wealth."

The reader learns the function before meeting the story.

Translations worth memorizing:

| Ungrounded | Grounded |
|---|---|
| "Meaningful experience" | "Behavioral studies, events, and community with a clear purpose" |
| "Fresh perspectives" | "Psychology frameworks most people never encounter" |
| "Interactive NPCs" | "Blue, an AI agent who reviews and rewards your work" |
| "Digital opportunities" | "Diamonds and cash bounties that belong to you" |

In story content — quest copy, character moments, Blue's dialogue — the narrative layer can stand alone when the reader already has product context. On brand and marketing surfaces, ground first. When unsure which surface you're on, treat it as marketing.

## Two rules that will feel wrong at first

Both contradict standard marketing training. Expect to break them by instinct for a few weeks.

### Technology stays backstage

The chain is a primitive, and it is never the pitch. Outward copy never names the infrastructure.

Banned in marketing: *Base, $BLUE, blockchain, onchain, crypto, token, wallet, mint, burn, gas, NFT, DEX, smart contract, transaction hash*, and any ticker. USDC included — write "cash" or "a dollar bounty."

Say what the reader gets: rewards that are real, permanent, and theirs. "Diamonds" and "credits" are the product nouns. If a sentence needs a chain term to land, it belongs in `docs/tokenomics/` or a technical surface.

### Never define us by contrast

Do not write "this isn't X, it's Y." State what a thing is and let it stand.

- No: "This isn't another meditation app — it's a research lab."
- Yes: "A research lab for personal development, with Blue as your co-investigator."

Negative framing is internal positioning only. It never reaches outward copy.

## Line-level habits

- **Delete hedge words.** Cut on sight: *kind of, sort of, maybe, possibly, somewhat, a bit, fairly, pretty, just, simply, basically, essentially, really, very, quite*. If the claim is too strong without the hedge, make a smaller true claim.
- **One idea per sentence.** Short sentences carry weight. Use periods.
- **Specifics over generics.** "Members" over "users." Name the actual reward, the actual surface, the actual agent. If you can't replace an abstraction with a number, name, date, verb, or noun, the abstraction is doing nothing.
- **Active voice, present tense.** "Blue reviews submissions" over "submissions are reviewed by Blue."
- **Cut "that" constructions.**
- **Numbers must be current and verifiable.** Never bake stats into reusable copy, and never invent one. Stale numbers read as lies the day they expire.
- **No all caps, anywhere.** Title Case is fine for feature titles. Her name is **Blue** in prose.
- **The cut test.** After a draft, count what you cut. If the second draft isn't roughly 30% shorter than the first, you proofread instead of editing. Go again.

## Habits to remove

Grep your own drafts for these families. Full list in `references/anti-patterns.md`.

- **Corporate wellness:** optimize your life, unlock your potential, your best self, holistic, empower, journey of self-discovery.
- **Referral bait:** invite your friends and earn, share to unlock, spots filling fast, don't miss out. We are not a growth funnel.
- **Vague self-help:** journey, awakening, manifest, alignment, frequency, higher self, best era.
- **Crypto-bro register:** WAGMI, gm, frens, anon, based, degen, diamond hands, to the moon. Zero of these belong in MWA copy.
- **Generic AI copy.** If the page describes something fifty other AI products also do, you've underdescribed.
- **Repeated information.** A heading, sub-heading, and intro that all say the same thing. Each layer adds new information.
- **"We are excited to announce."** Just announce it.
- **Mechanism-as-title.** Titles name the category the reader is entering; mechanism goes in the body. "A Membership You Own Forever" over "One NFT, Full Access."

## Before you ship

From the `EDITORIAL.md` revision checklist:

- Can someone explain what we do after reading this?
- Is the point certain and confident?
- Does every sentence deliver one clear thought or emotion?
- Have I named tangible benefits the reader can picture?
- Is every poetic phrase grounded in something concrete?
- Does the order go real, then story layer?
- One main idea per paragraph?
- Did I cut every sentence that sounds cool and means nothing?
- Did I avoid contrast framing?
- No recruiter bait, no corporate-wellness or vague self-help tells?
- No chain, token, or wallet vocabulary on a marketing surface?
- Would an investor understand this? Would an artist feel it?

Any "no" sends you back to the relevant reference.

## Using the skill in Claude Code

`mwa-editorial` is user-invocable as `/mwa-editorial`.

Draft first, then run it against what you wrote. Copy generated from a cold prompt converges toward sameness and teaches you little about the voice. Using the skill to critique your own draft builds judgment, and the checklist gives you language for why an edit was made.

One distinction that catches everyone: `mwa-editorial` governs brand voice, and `mwa-blue` governs Blue's in-character dialogue. They are separate voices, and both skills contain a file named `references/voice.md`. Writing as Blue means using `mwa-blue`.

The skill covers words. Visual, layout, and typography decisions live in the `EDITORIAL.md` visual identity section.
